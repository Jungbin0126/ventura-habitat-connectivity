# Identifying Habitat Connectivity Gaps Using Wildlife Roadkill Patterns
Spatial analysis of wildlife roadkill observations, habitat connectivity, and transportation infrastructure in Ventura County, California.

This project combines GIS analysis, spatial statistics, and ecological connectivity modeling to identify areas where wildlife movement may be disrupted by road networks and urban development.


## Research Overview
Habitat fragmentation caused by roads and urban expansion is a major threat to wildlife movement and biodiversity conservation. Wildlife roadkill observations can serve as indicators of ecological connectivity gaps where animals are forced to cross transportation corridors.

This study investigates spatial relationships between:
- Wildlife roadkill observations
- Transportation infrastructure
- Habitat suitability
- Ecological corridor connectivity

using geospatial analysis and environmental datasets from Ventura County, California.


## Methods
The project includes:
- Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) hotspot analysis
- Road distance analysis
- Spatial buffering
- Habitat suitability mapping
- Wildlife corridor analysis
- Random point comparison testing
- Wilcoxon statistical testing
- GIS visualization and mapping
